Learning Spanish 1

16 May 2015 — posted in writing

Last week I decided to lean Spanish, roughly according to the study plan of Tommo Valkonen. First of, some history about why I want to do this:

My interest in languages started when I was some time into learning Esperanto and I found out that such endeavors – learning a language – are not necessarily horrible. I began to play around with foreign grammar and looked into some other constructed languages and finally Icelandic, which I learned – and still learn – to some extend. However, I soon met the desire to speak a more conventionally useful language and began to study Arabic in parallel to Icelandic and a full university schedule. This was obviously not planned through and I soon dropped it. So, what easy but useful languages are out there? The list is quite short: Spanish, French, and Polish for family reasons. I don’t currently want to put up with French spelling and pronunciation, which leaves the other two. Reading the memrise blog post mentioned above, I decided that a 6 week programme fits right into my schedule: starting on May 8th and ending on June 25th, with a week of interruption to go cycling in Norway.

According to the plan, I first learn the alphabet and pronunciation of the language (yup, took an hour or so). The next step is to learn the most common words, so that I have a base for further learning. After that, I’ll learn basic grammar and create some sentences. The rest of the time, practice with real people over the internet. As an introvert, this will be the hardest part for me, I really hope I’m going to follow through with this.

Last week, I used memrise to learn the 1000 most common Spanish words. I heavily recommend memrise for vocab learning, but even for oft-learned languages like Spanish, not all top listed courses are really good. The number of learners is a good first indication of course quality, but I recommend looking at the course forum to find out how the course creator reacts to correction proposals. A good course also provides information about the words (e.g. gender), but doesn’t force you to always type the relevant markers and accepts alternative versions of words and other words with the same meaning. For learning, I recommend practicing daily (with obvious exceptions for vacations and special events). This is independent of your time frame to learn the language. If you really don’t find motivation to learn on a specific date, just repeat one or two words and call it a day.

I don’t know when I’ll publish the next progress report, because there will be a lot to do at the end of the next week. Final presentation for Bachelor’s degree, preparation for holiday, and all. Expect something by June 4th. I published this post without corrections, because I wouldn’t publish it at all would I force myself to make it perfect. Nonetheless, mayme you found it interesting or even motivating to start learning a language yourself.
